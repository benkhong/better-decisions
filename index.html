Module · One
01

How the mind misleads you.

Your intuitions are unreliable in predictable ways. Here's why — and what to do about it.

By the end of this module

You'll be able to name two specific cognitive failures by their proper names, catch your brain doing each of them on a fresh example, and explain why "just be more careful" doesn't fix any of it.

01.1Built for a different world

Your brain is an extraordinary machine. It recognizes faces in milliseconds, navigates complex social dynamics, and processes sensory information at speeds no computer can match. But it was built for a different world — one of immediate physical threats, small tribal groups, and concrete, observable reality.

For beginnersYou don't need to memorize the bias names. What matters is the habit of catching your brain when it feels certain on a question where it has no business being certain.

The modern world demands something very different: evaluating probabilities, reasoning about invisible risks, thinking in large numbers, and making decisions under radical uncertainty. We are, as one researcher put it, powerful parallel-processing engines designed for social navigation, but terribly built for manipulating abstract symbols.

This mismatch isn't a character flaw. It's a design constraint. Evolution optimized your ancestors for survival on the African savannah roughly 200,000 years ago, not for evaluating stock portfolios or choosing between career paths. The result is a brain that makes systematic errors in predictable directions — errors you can learn to see, and then correct.

01.2Two speeds of thinking

Your brain operates in two fundamentally different modes. The fast mode is intuitive, automatic, and effortless — it's what recognizes faces, reads emotions, and tells you to duck when something flies at your head. It handles roughly 95% of daily life brilliantly.

The slow mode is deliberate, calculating, and effortful — it's what you use for mental arithmetic, careful analysis, and weighing complex tradeoffs. It's powerful but lazy. It takes real metabolic energy to engage, which is why your brain defaults to fast mode whenever it can get away with it.

Heads upKahneman's "System 1 / System 2" framing is a useful metaphor, not literal brain anatomy. Don't take the map for the territory.

The problem: fast mode fails catastrophically on exactly the questions that matter most for decisions — probability, risk, complex tradeoffs. And because it operates automatically, you rarely notice when it's steering you wrong.

Example · The Linda problem

Linda is thirty-one years old, single, outspoken, and very bright. She majored in philosophy. As a student, she was deeply concerned with issues of discrimination and social justice.

Which is more probable? (A) Linda is a bank teller. (B) Linda is a bank teller and is active in the feminist movement.

Roughly 85% of participants chose B in Kahneman and Tversky's original 1983 study. But it's mathematically impossible — "bank teller AND feminist" is always a subset of "bank teller." B can never be more likely than A. People choose it because the story is more coherent, and our brains prioritize narrative plausibility over mathematical consistency.

Example · The malaria test

1% of a country's population has malaria. A test gives an accurate result 90% of the time. You test positive. What's the probability you actually have malaria?

If you guessed 90%, you've ignored the base rate. In a population of 1,000: ~10 actually have malaria (9 test positive correctly), and ~990 don't (99 get false positives). Total positives: 108. Real probability: about 8.3% — an order of magnitude below what most people guess, including many doctors. We'll come back to this with a calculator in Module 02.

01.3Anchoring — see it happen to you

One of the strangest things about your brain is how easily a completely irrelevant number can move your estimate of something else. In Kahneman and Tversky's classic 1974 experiment, participants spun a rigged wheel that landed on either 10 or 65. Then they were asked: What percentage of African countries are in the UN?

Common mistake

Reading the Linda problem, feeling embarrassed at being "tricked," and treating it as a one-off party trick. The whole point is that the same machinery runs constantly — when you read the news, when you size up a colleague, when you decide whether to trust a memory. Linda is a microscope, not a magic trick.

Mental model · The map is not the territory

Every model, framework, and shortcut in this manual is a simplification. No single lens captures reality. The drunkard searches for his keys under the streetlamp not because he dropped them there, but because the light is better. The goal isn't to find the One True Model — it's to have enough lenses to triangulate from multiple angles.

01.4A note on the replication crisis

Several famous results in behavioral economics have not held up under scrutiny. Ego depletion (the idea that willpower is a finite battery), large-scale priming effects, and several power-posing and stereotype-threat findings have either failed to replicate or shrunk dramatically once researchers tightened their methods. In 2017, Kahneman himself acknowledged he had "placed too much faith in underpowered studies" in Thinking, Fast and Slow.

The big-picture biases survive replication: anchoring, base-rate neglect, framing effects, loss aversion, overconfidence. The fine texture of specific studies does not. The lesson isn't to throw out the field — it's to hold every confident claim, including the ones in this manual, with a little less grip than you would if you didn't know about the crisis. Calibration applies to the literature too.

Why this mattersIf you've ever felt a flicker of doubt about a TED-talk-famous result, trust it. Healthy doubt is the skill being trained here.

You now know the software is buggy. Here's the upgraded operating system.

Try this today

Pick one belief you hold with high confidence — about a person, a market, a piece of news. Ask: "What specifically would have to be true for this belief to be wrong, and have I checked it?" Most strong beliefs collapse on first contact with that question.

Module · Two
02

Probability & evidence.

Before you can decide well, you need to understand how evidence actually works. Probability, Bayes, and the patterns that reveal signal in a noisy world.

By the end of this module

You'll be able to quantify how new evidence should change your beliefs, recognize three distinct ways "correlation ≠ causation" shows up, and know when to trust an average versus when you're in fat-tailed territory.

02.1Updating your beliefs

Every belief you hold is probabilistic — never 0%, never 100%. The discipline of good thinking is updating your beliefs proportionally to how diagnostic the new evidence actually is. Not too stubborn (anchoring to your first impression), not too reactive (overweighting whatever you heard last).

When new information arrives, the right question is: "How much more likely would I be to see this evidence in the world where my belief is true, versus the world where it's false?" If the evidence is equally likely in both worlds, it tells you nothing. If it's dramatically more likely in one, update significantly.

The whole gameOne sentence to keep: evidence is only as strong as the ratio between how expected it would be if you're right versus if you're wrong.
The equivalent bet test

A fast way to check if you actually believe what you claim to believe: translate it to a bet. "I'm 90% sure we'll hit our target" — would you bet $900 to win $100? If that feels too risky, your true confidence is lower than your stated confidence. Most people's "80% confident" crumbles under this test.

02.2Bayes, properly

The Linda and malaria problems above are Bayes problems. The full statement in plain English: your posterior belief (after evidence) is your prior belief (before evidence) multiplied by the likelihood ratio — how much more probable the evidence is in a world where you're right versus a world where you're wrong.

posterior odds   =   prior odds   ×   likelihood ratio
where   LR   =   P(evidence | hypothesis true)   /   P(evidence | hypothesis false)

The likelihood ratio is the part most people skip. A "positive test" sounds damning until you ask: how often does this test also come back positive when the person doesn't have the disease? An LR of 10 is a strong update. An LR of 2 is a nudge. An LR of 1 is noise dressed as signal.

You don't need to do this on paper for every belief — that would be exhausting. The practical move is to internalize the structure: prior + likelihood ratio = posterior. When someone presents you with a striking piece of evidence, the reflex question becomes "how often would this happen anyway?" — and that one question kills more bad conclusions than any other.

02.3Signal vs noise

The world generates more data every day, but very little of it is information. We are pattern-seeking animals — when information is sparse, we see patterns that don't exist. Horoscopes, superstitions, the "hot hand" in basketball (well, mostly — see below), stock-chart reading: noise dressed up as signal.

The practical discipline: in noisy domains (which is most of the real world), simple models beat complex ones. The forecaster who uses three variables outperforms the one who uses thirty — because the extra variables are fitting noise, not signal.

A real twist · The hot hand reversal

For thirty years, the "hot hand fallacy" — the idea that streak-shooting in basketball is an illusion — was a textbook example of how humans see patterns in randomness. In 2018, Joshua Miller and Adam Sanjurjo showed the original 1985 analysis contained a subtle statistical error. Correctly analyzed, players really do shoot better after a string of makes. The illusion was in the analysis, not in the streaks.

This is not a license to trust your gut on patterns. It is a reminder that the literature itself needs the same Bayesian treatment as everything else. Sometimes the consensus is the bias.

02.4Correlation is not causation

This is the foundational statistical sin, and even very smart people commit it constantly. When A and B move together, there are at least four possibilities: A causes B, B causes A, some hidden third thing C causes both, or it's pure coincidence.

Three ways correlation lies

Confounders. Ice cream sales and drowning deaths spike together every summer. Neither causes the other — summer heat drives both.

Reverse causation. Married people are healthier than unmarried people. Does marriage improve health? Or do healthier people marry more easily? Probably both — but the intervention implications are completely different.

Simpson's paradox. UC Berkeley was sued for gender discrimination because overall admission rates were higher for men. But examined department by department, women were admitted at equal or higher rates in most departments. Women applied disproportionately to competitive departments, skewing the aggregate. The "overall" number was misleading.

02.5The narrative fallacy

Humans compulsively construct causal stories from random sequences. The story always "makes sense" in retrospect, which tricks us into thinking we understood it — or could have predicted it — all along.

Every post-mortem is contaminated by this. The past looks ordered because we impose narrative on it. "The 2008 crisis happened because of subprime mortgages, deregulation, and greed." This sounds explanatory. But in 2006, the same facts existed and almost no one predicted the crisis. The narrative was constructed after the fact.

TestRead a confident historical explanation. Ask: would someone with this information in 1995 have predicted what happened? If not, the explanation is a story, not a model.

We tell ourselves stories explaining our actions, but are they all illusions? In reality, there are many overlapping stories for any single action.

Nassim Nicholas Taleb · The Black Swan

02.6Regression to the mean

Extreme results are partly skill and partly luck. Since the luck portion doesn't repeat, extreme performances are naturally followed by less extreme ones. This is not a mysterious force — it's simple statistics. But we chronically mistake it for a real phenomenon.

The Sports Illustrated "cover jinx": athletes on the cover tend to perform worse afterward. Not because of a curse — they made the cover because of an unusually good stretch. Some of that was luck, and luck doesn't persist.

Why this matters for decisions

If you reward someone after an exceptional quarter and they regress, you'll conclude the reward was irrelevant. If you punish someone after a terrible quarter and they improve, you'll conclude the punishment worked. Both are just regression to the mean. Over time, you'll systematically conclude that punishment works and reward doesn't — which is exactly backwards.

02.7Fat tails — when averages lie

Heights follow a bell curve — most people cluster around the average, and extreme heights are vanishingly rare. This is because your height doesn't affect my height. The events are independent.

But wealth, book sales, city sizes, startup returns, viral content, and pandemic deaths follow fat-tailed distributions — where events are causally linked. More popular songs get more notice, which makes them even more popular. The rich get richer. The extreme event dominates the total.

Bell curve vs fat tail
Same axes, completely different game.

Put Bill Gates in a room with 99 random Americans, and the "average" net worth is ~$1 billion. That tells you about one person, not the room. If you're operating in a fat-tailed domain, strategies built on averages will fail. Venture capital doesn't need most investments to work — one 100× return drives the fund. Risk management can't use standard deviation — a single event can exceed all previous events combined.

Common mistake

People confidently apply bell-curve intuitions to fat-tailed domains: career income, social-media reach, the next epidemic, AI breakthroughs. "It hasn't happened in the last ten years, so it won't" is sound reasoning in a bell-curve world and catastrophic reasoning in a fat-tailed one. Know which world you're in before you reach for a tool.

Module · Three
03

Honest self-assessment.

Your biases are predictable and your calibration is measurable. Here's how to see your own reasoning more clearly.

By the end of this module

You'll know the biases most likely to mislead you, be able to measure your own calibration, and have tools for genuinely testing your beliefs rather than just defending them.

03.1Key biases & their antidotes

Anchoring. The first number you encounter biases all subsequent estimates — even when it's completely irrelevant. You just saw this happen to you in the Mongolia widget above. Antidote: generate your own estimate before seeing anyone else's.

Loss aversion & prospect theory. Losses hurt roughly 2× more than equivalent gains feel good. But it goes deeper: we're risk-seeking in the domain of losses and risk-averse in the domain of gains. This is why founders whose startups are failing take increasingly reckless bets ("we're already down, might as well swing big") while successful founders play it too safe.

Prospect theory · the value function
The kink at zero is loss aversion. The curvature is what makes people irrational on both sides.

Availability heuristic. We overweight vivid, recent, or emotionally charged information. After 9/11, many Americans switched from flying to driving. The resulting increase in car-accident deaths — an estimated 1,595 additional fatalities — exceeded the number who died on the four hijacked flights. The vivid low-probability risk displaced the mundane high-probability one.

Survivorship bias. We study successes because failures are invisible. "College dropouts like Gates and Zuckerberg became billionaires, so maybe dropping out is viable." For every dropout billionaire, there are millions you never hear about who earn less than graduates. During WWII, the military examined bullet holes on returning bombers to decide where to add armor. Abraham Wald realized: the holes show where planes can take damage and survive. The armor was needed where the holes weren't — those planes never came back.

Confirmation bias. We search for evidence that confirms our beliefs, not evidence that tests them. This is doubly dangerous because it's invisible — you feel like you're doing research when you're really building a case.

The lollapalooza effect. Charlie Munger's term for when 3-5 biases push the same direction simultaneously. The combined force is explosive — far greater than any single bias. The dot-com bubble: social proof + envy + incentive-caused bias + loss aversion. No single bias explains the mania; the combination does. Whenever you see extreme collective behavior — bubbles, panics, cults — look for the lollapalooza.

2026 lensRecent crypto cycles, NFT mania, and AI hype waves all fit the pattern. Not because the underlying tech is fake, but because the lollapalooza overshoots the underlying truth by an order of magnitude.

03.3Calibration — knowing what you don't know

When you say "I'm 80% sure," are you right 80% of the time? Research by Philip Tetlock found that most people's 90%-confident predictions come true only about 70% of the time. We are systematically overconfident.

Calibration — the skill of making your stated confidence match your actual accuracy — is trainable. It's the single biggest differentiator between superforecasters (the top 2% of predictors) and everyone else. The practice is simple: make specific predictions with explicit confidence levels, write them down, then track how often you're right at each confidence level.

An underrated benefit: granularity forces thinking. Asking "Is this 60% likely or 65%?" forces deeper analysis than "Is this likely or unlikely?" Coarse buckets let you hide from precision.

03.4Forecasting tournaments & Brier scores

Until about 2010, calibration was something you trained alone in a notebook. That has changed. Public forecasting platforms — Metaculus, Manifold Markets, the Good Judgment Open — let anyone bet (with play money or real money) on real-world questions and see their score against thousands of other forecasters. This is the closest thing we have to a gym for the skill of being right.

The standard scoring is the Brier score: the mean squared error between your probability and the outcome (1 if it happened, 0 if it didn't). A perfect forecaster has a Brier of 0. Always saying 50% gives 0.25. Top superforecasters score around 0.15. The point isn't the number — it's that the number exists, gets worse when you bullshit, and gets better when you actually think.

Try onePick three real-world questions on a forecasting site this week. Put numbers on them. You'll learn more in a month than in a year of reading.

Tetlock's "ten commandments" of superforecasters compress to a few habits worth memorizing: triage your effort to questions you can actually move on, break hard problems into tractable sub-questions, balance the inside view (this specific case) with the outside view (the reference class), update often but in small steps, look hard for the strongest case against your view, and distinguish your degree of confidence from the degree of consensus.

Calibration self-test

For each question, give a 90% confidence interval — a low and high estimate you're 90% sure contains the right answer. If you're well-calibrated, you'll get about 9 out of 10 right. Most people get 3 to 5. Be honest; the test is for you.

03.5The outside view

This is perhaps the single most powerful debiasing technique. Instead of building up your estimate from the specific details of your situation (the "inside view"), start with how similar situations actually turned out historically (the "outside view").

"My startup will succeed because our product is great" — inside view. "90% of startups fail. What specifically makes us an exception?" — outside view.

The Sydney Opera House was estimated at 4 years and $7 million. It took 16 years and $102 million. That's not unusual — that's the base rate for megaprojects. Kahneman himself was on a team estimating completion of a curriculum project. His team's inside estimate: 2 years. The reference class of similar projects: 7-10 years average. It took 8 years.

The 2023 updateBent Flyvbjerg's How Big Things Get Done turned the outside view into a methodology: "reference-class forecasting." For any project, find the empirical distribution of how similar projects actually went, and start your estimate from that distribution, not from your plan.

The deeper point: when you're forecasting your own situation, your brain is generating an inside-view estimate by default, with all of the optimism that entails. The outside view is not a different opinion — it's a different method. Use both, and weight the outside view harder than feels comfortable.

03.6Soldier vs scout mindset

Julia Galef draws a sharp distinction between two reasoning modes. The soldier treats reasoning as defensive combat: "Can I believe this? Must I believe this?" The goal is to defend existing positions. The scout treats reasoning as mapmaking: "Is this true?" The goal is accuracy, even when it's uncomfortable.

The deepest insight: the root cause of soldier mindset isn't lazy thinking — it's identity. When a belief becomes part of who you are ("I'm the kind of person who…"), updating it feels like self-destruction. The more you've staked your identity on a position, the harder it becomes to see evidence against it.

Quick tests for motivated reasoning

· "If my political opponent held this view, would I find the arguments convincing?"

· "If the evidence pointed the other way, would I accept it?"

· "If I had zero ego investment in this position, what would I believe?"

03.7Steelmanning & the Ideological Turing Test

The move beyond scout mindset: actively construct the strongest version of the position you disagree with. This is steelmanning — the opposite of strawmanning. The bar is not "can I describe their view?" but "could a smart proponent of their view recognize my summary as the strongest version of their case?"

Bryan Caplan's Ideological Turing Test formalizes this: can you state the opposing view so convincingly that a sincere proponent would mistake you for one of them? If you can't, you don't understand the position well enough to disagree with it productively. You're disagreeing with a caricature.

Hard versionFor one belief you hold strongly, write a 500-word essay arguing the opposite — good enough that someone who actually holds that view would nod along. Most people find this nearly impossible. That's the diagnosis.
Epistemic learned helplessness

Scott Alexander's observation: smart people are dangerous to themselves because they can be argued into almost anything. A brilliant rhetorician with a wrong view can run circles around you in real time — and feeling outmatched in conversation is not evidence that they're correct. Most stable adults eventually develop a learned helplessness around clever arguments: "I can't refute it, but I'm also not going to update much on it." This is the right move when you're outside your circle of competence. It's not anti-intellectual; it's a base-rate response to the fact that being out-argued has almost no correlation with being wrong.

You now have sharper perception than 95% of people. But seeing clearly isn't the same as choosing well. Models describe reality — they don't tell you what to do. Let's cross from thinking to deciding.

Try this today

Open Metaculus or Manifold Markets. Find three questions resolving in the next 90 days that you care about. Put a probability on each — not "likely / unlikely" but a number. Set a reminder to check the outcomes. You're now a forecaster.

Module · Four
04

Evaluating options.

The analytical frameworks for sizing up what's in front of you — probabilities, payoffs, risks, and how much to stake.

By the end of this module

You'll be able to separate decision quality from outcome quality, calculate expected value, size a bet so you can't be ruined, and know when a decision deserves your full attention versus a quick call.

04.1Resulting

Here is the core principle of good decision-making: decisions are the product of skill and luck. That's all there is to it.

Resulting is the tendency to evaluate a decision based on its outcome, without accounting for luck. We do this constantly and unconsciously. If a decision worked out, it was a "good decision." If it didn't, it was bad. But this confuses two very different things.

Decision quality × outcome — the resulting matrix

The poker analogy makes this crystal clear. A good poker player doesn't ask "did I win this hand?" They ask "did I make a good bet given what I knew?" Going all-in with pocket aces and losing to a lucky flush draw — the decision was perfect. The outcome was bad. If you change your strategy because you lost, you're resulting.

Common mistake

Reviewing a past decision and concluding "I should have known better" because you can see the outcome. You couldn't have known. The post-hoc clarity is the narrative fallacy working its way back into your evaluation. Ask instead: given the information available at the time, was this the right move?

04.2Thinking in bets & expected value

Here's the fundamental reframe: every decision you make is a bet, whether you think of it that way or not. When you choose a career path, you're wagering years of your life against an uncertain outcome. When you decide to have a difficult conversation, you're betting short-term discomfort against potential long-term improvement. When you choose not to act, you're betting that the status quo will serve you better than the alternative.

Most people don't think this way. They think of bets as things that happen in casinos. But the structure is identical: you're putting something at stake (time, money, energy, opportunity cost) against an uncertain outcome with some probability of different payoffs. Once you see this, the question changes from "what should I do?" to "is this a good bet?"

The three layers

Layer 1 · Expand all future possibilities. Before analyzing anything, map the full range of possible outcomes — not just the ones that come to mind first. What could go right? What could go wrong? What could happen that you haven't even considered? Most people fixate on one or two scenarios. The discipline is seeing the full tree.

Layer 2 · Assign probabilities and payoffs. For each possibility, estimate two things: how likely is it (probability), and what's it worth if it happens (payoff)? Expected value is the probability-weighted sum across all outcomes.

EV   =   Σ   (probability × payoff)   for each outcome

A decision with 70% chance of gaining $50K and 30% chance of losing $10K:
EV = (0.7 × $50,000) + (0.3 × −$10,000) = $35,000 − $3,000 = +$32,000.
This is a good bet — as long as you can absorb the $10K loss if it happens.

Layer 3 · Document your knowledge state. Before you decide, write down what you know, what you're uncertain about, and what you don't know at all. This is your knowledge graph — and it's what separates a thoughtful bet from a blind one. It also gives you something to review later when you're evaluating whether the decision was good, independent of the outcome.

Portfolio thinking

The EV mindset gets even more powerful when you zoom out from individual decisions to your portfolio of decisions. Every initiative in your life is a bet on the future with three variables: potential upside, cost, and confidence.

The insight: manage your life decisions as a portfolio, not as individual bets. You need a mix. High-confidence, low-upside bets (steady job, index funds, maintaining existing relationships) give you stability. Low-confidence, high-upside bets (side projects, career pivots, bold conversations) give you the chance of a step-change. A portfolio of only "safe" bets guarantees mediocrity. A portfolio of only wild bets guarantees ruin. The art is in the balance.

Everything is a bet. Decisions based on expected value. Knowing when decisions need analytical rigour and when some things are a matter of taste — that's where wisdom lives.

Annie Duke · Thinking in Bets

When EV breaks down · the variance problem

Two bets can have the same expected value with wildly different risk profiles. A guaranteed $100 and a 1% chance of $10,000 both have EV = $100. But if you only get to play once, they are very different decisions.

This is the fundamental tension: EV is a guide for repeated decisions, not one-shot bets. A venture capitalist can afford to lose on 9 out of 10 investments because the one winner covers everything. You, making one career decision, cannot afford the same calculus. The question isn't just "is this positive EV?" but also "how many times do I get to play this game, and can I survive the losses along the way?"

This connects directly to risk of ruin below. The first rule of EV thinking: make sure the downside doesn't end the game.

04.3One-way vs two-way doors

Jeff Bezos draws a distinction that instantly clarifies how much effort any decision deserves. Two-way doors are reversible — if it doesn't work, you can walk back through. Make these fast, with about 70% of the information you wish you had. One-way doors are irreversible — you can't easily undo them. These deserve deep, careful analysis.

The insight: roughly 90% of decisions are two-way doors that people agonize over unnecessarily. Choosing a restaurant, trying a new workflow, launching a small experiment — these are all reversible. Signing a 5-year lease, choosing a co-founder, having a child — these are not. Match your decision effort to the stakes.

For beginnersIf a decision is bothering you and the answer to "could I reverse this in 3 months?" is yes — you are overthinking. Make a call, learn from how it goes, adjust.

04.4Risk of ruin

If a bad outcome can permanently knock you out of the game, no positive expected value justifies the bet. This is the most practically important concept in risk management.

Consider a 60/40 coin flip where you bet your entire net worth each round. Positive EV — but you'll eventually go broke with certainty, because you only need to lose once. The same logic applies to using maximum leverage in trading, startups that burn all cash on a single bet, and any strategy where a single failure is unrecoverable.

The first rule is: don't go bust. No matter how good the opportunity looks, if the worst case ends the game, walk away.

Nassim Nicholas Taleb

The deeper concept is ergodicity: what happens on average across many people (the ensemble) is not the same as what happens to one person over time. Russian roulette has positive expected value across the group, but no individual should play. Whenever ruin, leverage, or compounding is involved, the ensemble average can be positive while the individual time-path leads to zero.

04.5Kelly criterion — how much to bet

Granted you've decided a bet is worth taking, how much should you wager? The Kelly criterion gives the mathematically optimal fraction of your bankroll to risk on any single bet, given the edge and the odds.

Kelly fraction   =   (p × b − q) / b
where   p = probability of winning,   q = 1 − p,   b = net payoff per unit risked

Kelly maximizes the long-run growth rate of your bankroll. Bet more than Kelly suggests and growth slows (and risk of ruin climbs). Bet less and growth slows but the ride is gentler. In practice, professional bettors and investors almost always use half-Kelly or quarter-Kelly — the math is right, but estimation error in p means full Kelly is more aggressive than your real edge can support. The widget above does the math for you.

Worth knowingThe same formula governs how much of your career capital to risk on any one project. Going "full Kelly" on a startup means going all-in. Half-Kelly means keeping a real fallback. Most successful career bettors are, mathematically, half-Kelly people.

04.6Sunk costs & status quo bias

These are the twin forces that keep people stuck in bad situations for years.

Sunk cost fallacy. Money, time, or effort already spent should not influence forward-looking decisions. "We've invested too much to stop now" — but the $5 million is gone either way. The only relevant question is: "Would I invest another dollar in this if I were starting from scratch today?" If the answer is no, the previous investment is irrelevant.

Status quo bias. People treat "doing nothing" as a non-decision, when it is in fact an active choice with its own expected value. Staying in a bad job isn't neutral — it's choosing to spend another year of your life there. Together, sunk costs and status quo bias explain why people stay in bad jobs, relationships, and investments far longer than they should.

04.7Kill criteria — Duke's update on sunk costs

The classic advice for sunk costs is "ignore them." This is correct and almost never works in practice, because by the time you're in the situation, your identity has fused with the project and you can no longer see clearly. Annie Duke's 2022 book Quit offers a fix that does work: set kill criteria before you start.

The move: while you're still cold and clear, decide the conditions under which you would walk away. "I'll give this venture 18 months and $200K of my savings; if revenue is under $X by month 12 and our top hire has left, I quit." Write it down. Tell someone. Then, when the moment arrives — and your in-the-moment self desperately wants to "give it another quarter" — the kill criteria are doing the work the calm version of you wanted done.

Why this is hardYou will feel like the criteria don't apply to your specific situation. That feeling is the bias firing. The criteria were written for that feeling.

The other half of the insight: quitting on time looks like quitting too early. If you wait until quitting is obviously correct, you have waited too long. The right time to quit is when the EV of continuing has gone negative — which is almost always before the situation is visibly hopeless.

04.8Optionality

When you can't predict the future — which is always — prefer choices that keep future doors open. Optionality means nonlinear upside with capped downside.

Taleb's barbell strategy: put 85-90% of your resources in ultra-safe positions and 10-15% in extremely speculative bets. Avoid the mediocre middle. The safe portion ensures survival; the speculative portion captures asymmetric upside. This applies far beyond finance — keep a stable income while taking wild swings on side projects. Protect the core while making bets that could change everything.

Module · Five
05

Making the call.

When to decide, how to manage the emotional dimensions, and the wisdom refined by decades of high-stakes decision-making.

By the end of this module

You'll know when you have enough information to act, how to apply inversion and regret minimization, and have internalized the principles that guide the most consistent long-term decision-makers.

05.1When to stop researching and decide

Herbert Simon's insight: satisficers set a threshold and pick the first option that clears it. Maximizers try to find the absolute best option. Counterintuitively, satisficers are typically happier and make decisions that are objectively about as good — because the marginal improvement from continued searching is usually less than the cost.

The 70% rule: decide when you have 70% of the information you wish you had. A good decision now usually beats a perfect decision later. The cost of delay is real but invisible.

Before gathering more data, ask: "Would any result change my decision?" If no result would change your course, the information has no decision value — and gathering it is pure procrastination.

05.2Inversion

Charlie Munger's favorite tool, borrowed from the mathematician Jacobi: "Invert, always invert." Instead of asking "how do I succeed?", ask "what would guarantee failure?" — and avoid those things.

The forward question is often impossibly broad. "How do I build a great company?" — hard. "What would definitely destroy one?" — easy: dishonest accounting, ignoring customers, hiring badly, running out of cash. Now just… don't do those things. The inverted question is almost always more tractable and more actionable.

05.3Regret minimization

For the class of decisions where EV analysis doesn't help — because the options are incommensurable — Bezos offers this: project yourself to age 80 and ask which choice minimizes regret.

This cuts through analysis paralysis by shifting from probability-weighted outcomes to identity-weighted outcomes. "Will I regret not trying this?" is a different question from "what's the expected return?" — and for career changes, relationships, and major life moves, it's often the more useful one.

05.4Emotional state awareness

Don't make consequential decisions while emotionally activated — angry, euphoric, anxious, or grieving. Your risk tolerance shifts in ways you wouldn't endorse when calm.

The 24-hour rule: sleep on any irreversible decision. The 10/10/10 framework: how will I feel about this in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years? Most bad decisions optimize for the 10-minute feeling. The 10-month frame almost always makes the right choice obvious.

05.5Second-order effects

First-order thinking: "What happens if I do X?" Second-order thinking: "And then what happens because of that?"

Rent control makes housing cheaper (first-order). Developers build less housing because returns are capped (second-order). Supply shrinks, making the shortage worse (third-order). The policy achieves the opposite of its intent at the second order.

Almost all competitive advantage in decision-making comes from consistently thinking one or two orders ahead. Most people don't get past the first.

05.6What the great investors know

There is a particular kind of decision wisdom that gets sharpened by allocating money, because the feedback is precise (the price tells you), the consequences are real (you lose actual capital), and the games run for decades. The investors who have done this best — Warren Buffett, Charlie Munger, Benjamin Graham, Howard Marks — talk about decisions in remarkably similar terms. The framing is finance-flavored, but the structure transfers to any decision where survival, patience, and consequences matter. Which is most of them.

For beginnersSubstitute "career capital," "social capital," or "the thing you're trying to build" for "money" and every idea below still works. Money is the cleanest training ground because the feedback is so precise — the lessons aren't really about money.

Circle of competence. Buffett's most-repeated idea: define honestly what you know and what you don't, and operate inside that boundary. "The size of that circle is not very important; knowing its boundaries, however, is vital." Almost every catastrophic decision is made just outside the decider's circle of competence, by someone who didn't realize they had crossed it. The practical move: write down five subjects where you can genuinely outperform an average smart person, and five where you can't but feel like you can. The trouble lives in the second list.

Margin of safety. Graham's foundational principle, inherited by Buffett. When you make a decision under uncertainty, don't aim for the boundary of what works — leave room for error. Build the bridge to carry 30,000 pounds and drive only 10,000-pound trucks across it. In investment this means buying a dollar for fifty cents. In hiring, choosing someone obviously above the bar, not just above it. In planning, assuming things take 50% longer than your honest estimate. The margin isn't pessimism; it's an explicit acknowledgment that you can't model everything.

Mr. Market. Graham's parable, beloved of Buffett. Imagine your business has a partner named Mr. Market who shows up every day with a price at which he'll buy you out or sell you more. Some days he's euphoric and quotes wild prices; some days he's despondent and offers you bargains. The right relationship is: trade with him only when his quotes suit you. The rest of the time, ignore him.

Most people invert this. They let Mr. Market's mood drive their decisions — buying when prices are rising (he's confident, he must be right) and selling when prices are falling (he's worried, he must know something). The discipline is to keep your own assessment of value separate from his stated price, and use the gap rather than be moved by it. The reframe extends well beyond stocks: every market — for jobs, for housing, for status, for ideas — has a Mr. Market component. Your job is not to follow his moods.

Worth knowingThe single hardest version of this is the housing market when everyone you know is buying. The crowd is loudest exactly when the price is wrong.

The patience premium. "The stock market is designed to transfer money from the active to the patient." This is a stronger claim than it sounds. In almost any competitive game with many participants, almost everyone is optimizing for the quarter, the news cycle, the next move. Almost no one is operating on a ten-year horizon. The patient player is competing in a near-empty field.

Munger sharpens it: "The first rule of compounding: never interrupt it unnecessarily." A 10% return compounded over thirty years multiplies your money seventeen-fold. The same 10% with one panic-sell at year fifteen and a year of sitting out is meaningfully less. Most of the wealth destruction over a lifetime comes not from picking wrong but from interrupting compounding — yanking money out at the bottom, swapping a working strategy for a fashionable one, blowing up a career trajectory with one impulsive move. The same logic governs anything else that compounds: skills, relationships, reputation, health.

The punch card. Buffett's thought experiment: imagine you went through life with a card that has twenty punches on it — one for each major investment decision you're ever allowed to make. You can never get more. How carefully would you choose each one? The exercise reveals how much capacity for action we waste on noise. If you knew you had twenty in a lifetime, you'd wait. You'd think. You'd say no a lot. The same constraint applied to careers, partnerships, major purchases, or public statements would change them dramatically. The punch card is a reminder that the cost of a decision isn't just its outcome — it's the implicit signal that something was worth a punch.

The "too hard" pile. Munger's clearest piece of advice: when you encounter a decision you can't confidently evaluate, put it in the "too hard" pile and move on. You don't have to have a view on everything. Most decisions presented to you don't need to be made by you — and pretending they do is how confident-sounding mistakes get made. The discipline is admitting "I don't know enough to have a real view," which is socially much harder than it sounds.

It is remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent.

Charlie Munger

Price is what you pay; value is what you get. Buffett's compression of the core move. Almost everyone watches the price. Almost no one independently estimates the value. The decoupling is where all the durable advantage comes from — in markets and out of them. The discipline of forming your own view of what something is worth, before you check what others are charging or paying, is the same discipline whether you're evaluating a stock, a job offer, a house, or a relationship.

Be greedy when others are fearful. The contrarian's discipline. Easier said than done because being greedy when others are fearful means making your move when everything in your social environment is telling you you're wrong. The point is not to be contrarian for its own sake — that's just a different form of conformity. The point is that crowd consensus tends to overshoot in both directions, and the best entry points feel uncomfortable. If you're never uncomfortable, you're never early.

Rule number one: don't lose money. Buffett's two rules — rule one, never lose money; rule two, never forget rule one — sound like a joke until you connect them to ergodicity and risk of ruin from earlier in this module. The asymmetry of returns and losses is brutal: a 50% loss requires a 100% gain to recover. A 90% loss requires a 900% gain. Avoiding the catastrophic loss is mathematically more valuable than capturing the spectacular gain, even when the gain has a higher expected value. Survive first, then optimize. This is the single principle that connects almost every other idea in this guide.

Reputation as a long-dated position. "It takes twenty years to build a reputation and five minutes to ruin it. If you think about that, you'll do things differently." Reputation compounds asymmetrically — slow upside, sudden downside. The decisions that protect it (don't lie, don't cut corners, don't take credit you didn't earn) compound for decades. The decisions that destroy it can fit in a single email. Treat your name as a long-dated position you're not allowed to liquidate.

Common mistake

Treating these as investment principles rather than decision principles. The Buffett shelf is full of books that translate his ideas into stock-picking systems. The ideas are about something much wider — how to handle uncertainty, patience, and consequences across a lifetime. The investor-readers who do best with this material are the ones who apply it to their careers, partnerships, and time first, and to their portfolios second.

Try this today

Pick one ongoing commitment in your life — a project, a relationship, a habit. Write down, right now, the conditions under which you'd walk away from it. Be specific: "If X is still true by Y date, I quit." That's a kill criterion. You just got better at sunk costs than 95% of people.

06

A working toolkit.

Practical instruments that survive contact with reality. Use these.

By the end of this module

You'll have six fast tests you can run on any decision in under a minute, two printable templates to use when stakes are high, six worked examples to model from, and a working method for using a language model as a thinking partner.

06.1Six quick tests for any decision

When a real decision is in front of you, you won't have time to read this manual. You need a few tests sharp enough to run in 60 seconds. These are the six I reach for most.

Test · 01
The Outsider Test
"What would I tell my closest friend to do?"

Strips away the emotional and ego components that are biasing you, instantly. You suddenly see the situation with the clarity reserved for other people's lives.

If your advice to them is different from your advice to yourself, the difference is the bias.
Test · 02
The Pre-Mortem
"It's a year from now and this failed. Why?"

Gary Klein's technique. By assuming failure as a fact, you unlock candid critique that hindsight bias normally suppresses. Generates a richer list of risks than "what could go wrong?"

Spend 5 minutes listing failure causes as if they already happened. The top 2 become things you actively design around.
Test · 03
The EV Bet Test
"Would I take this as a 100×-repeated bet?"

Translates the decision into a wager. Forces you to put numbers on probability and payoff. Reveals whether you actually believe what you're saying or just feel optimistic.

If you wouldn't repeat it 100 times in identical situations, you're not actually confident — you're hoping.
Test · 04
The NASA Checklist
"What am I assuming that might not be true?"

Write down the three to five load-bearing assumptions behind your plan. List them explicitly. Most failures trace to one assumption that everyone in the room treated as given.

Then ask of each: "If this turns out false, what's my plan?" Now you've stress-tested.
Test · 05
Inversion
"What would guarantee failure?"

Munger's tool. The forward question ("how do I succeed?") is impossibly broad. The inverted one yields a tractable list — and you just have to not do those things.

Write the failure list. Then check: am I currently doing any of these? You'd be surprised how often the answer is yes.
Test · 06
The One-Way Door Test
"Can I reverse this in 3 months?"

Match the effort to the stakes. If yes, decide in 70% of the time you think you need. If no, the decision deserves your most careful thinking — and probably a pre-mortem.

If you can't decide whether it's one-way or two-way, that ambiguity is itself information — treat as one-way to be safe.

06.2The decision journal

The single highest-leverage habit in this whole manual. Before any consequential decision, write down: the decision, the situation, your alternatives, the relevant information, your reasoning, your prediction with confidence levels. Then revisit when you know the outcome.

This compounds in three ways. It improves your decisions in the moment — the act of writing forces structured thinking and exposes weak reasoning. It protects against hindsight bias — you can actually see what you believed at the time, not what your brain rewrote later. It builds calibration — over months and years, you learn whether your 70%-confidents are actually 70%.

06.5Using a language model as a thinking partner

A language model — Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini — is not a source of truth. But it is a remarkably good thinking partner for the techniques in this manual. Used well, it compresses days of structured thinking into an afternoon. Used badly, it amplifies your existing biases by telling you confidently what you wanted to hear.

The trick is to set it up as an adversarial collaborator, not a sycophantic one. The default mode of these models is to help; you have to explicitly tell them to push back. Four prompt patterns that consistently produce better thinking:

Prompt · The steel-man

"I'm leaning toward [decision] because [reasons]. Steel-man the opposite view as if you genuinely held it. Don't soften it. The best version of 'don't do this.' Then identify which of my reasons would collapse first under that argument."

Prompt · The pre-mortem

"It's twelve months from now. This decision failed badly. Write the post-mortem — what went wrong, in what order, and which warning signs I likely ignored. Be specific and uncharitable. Don't hedge."

Prompt · The calibration check

"Here are my probability estimates for [outcomes]: [list]. Identify where these probabilities seem internally inconsistent, where I'm likely overconfident, and what reference classes I should be checking against."

Prompt · The reference class

"For [my situation], what's the reference class of similar situations historically? What's the base rate of success? What predicts which cases ended up in the success tail, separately from the founder/operator's self-narrative?"

The trap to watch for

Language models are trained, in part, to please. If you describe a plan with conviction, the model will help you execute it — even when the right move is to challenge the plan itself. Counteract this by asking for the disagreement first, before asking for the help. Set the model's role explicitly: "Act as a skeptical investor evaluating this pitch." Roles work.

Try this today

Take one decision you're currently weighing. Open Claude or ChatGPT. Paste the steel-man prompt above with your actual situation. Read the answer carefully. Notice which of your reasons feel shakiest after — those are the ones that need more work, not less.

Module · Seven
07

The forces shaping every decision.

Your decisions happen inside systems where other agents adapt. Here's the level above individual choice.

By the end of this module

You'll see why most apparent "individual choices" are determined by the game being played around them, why optimization sometimes destroys the thing being optimized, and how to build a life that gains from disorder rather than being broken by it.

07.1Incentives explain almost everything

Charlie Munger again: "Show me the incentive, and I'll show you the outcome." Most apparent stupidity, malice, or irrationality dissolves once you map the incentives the actors are actually facing. People rarely act against their incentives for long.

This isn't cynicism — it's a diagnostic. If you find yourself thinking "why on earth would they do that?", you're looking at a system whose incentives you haven't mapped yet. Map them and the behavior usually becomes obvious.

Goodhart's Law: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." The moment people know they're being judged on a metric, they optimize for the metric rather than the thing the metric was supposed to track. Standardized testing produced test-prep instead of education. Citation counts produced citation gaming. Engagement metrics produced rage-bait. Every measurement system corrodes itself over time.

DefenseWhen designing systems: never tie significant rewards to a single metric. Always measure 2-3 things that pull in different directions. The optimization toward one creates pressure on the others, exposing the gaming.

Skin in the game. Taleb's principle: people who bear no consequences from their advice should not be taken seriously. The forecaster who never gets penalized for being wrong, the consultant who's gone before the strategy plays out, the executive with a golden parachute — their incentives are pure asymmetry. Adjust your trust accordingly. Conversely, when evaluating your own advice to others, ask: "Am I bearing any cost if I'm wrong?" If not, your confidence should be lower than it feels.

07.2Basic game theory

You are rarely deciding in isolation. Most of your decisions are moves in a game where other agents are also choosing, often in response to you. A handful of game-theory concepts will sharpen this.

Nash equilibrium: a state where no player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing strategy. Most stable real-world situations sit at Nash equilibria — including bad ones. Traffic jams, arms races, status competitions. Nobody benefits from defecting alone, even when everyone would benefit if everyone changed at once. This is why coordination problems are so hard: rational individual action produces irrational collective outcomes.

Tragedy of the commons. Individually rational extraction of a shared resource leads to its collective destruction. Overfishing, antibiotic resistance, climate change, attention markets. Solutions exist (property rights, regulation, social norms, Elinor Ostrom's work on community management) but they require coordination — which is exactly what the tragedy makes hard.

Repeated games change everything. A single-shot interaction often rewards defection: take the favor, don't reciprocate, walk away. A repeated interaction does not. The shadow of future encounters makes cooperation the dominant strategy. Tit-for-tat — cooperate first, then copy whatever they did last — wins iterated prisoner's dilemma tournaments. If you're playing one-shot games when you could be playing repeated ones, you're leaving enormous value on the table.

The Red Queen effect. In any competitive arena, running stands still while others run too. If your competition is improving 5% per year and you're improving 5% per year, your relative position is unchanged. To advance, you need to outpace the field — not just improve in absolute terms. Most fields run faster than you think.

07.3Moloch — coordination failure at scale

Scott Alexander's essay Meditations on Moloch describes the most important pattern in modern civilization: multipolar traps where each player, acting rationally on their own incentives, contributes to an outcome no one would have chosen. There's no villain. There's just the structure of the game.

Social media optimizes for engagement, which optimizes for outrage, which makes everyone more miserable but no individual platform can stop because users will go to whichever competitor doesn't. Academic publishing optimizes for citation counts, which optimizes for incremental papers, which makes the science less useful but no individual researcher can fix it. Companies optimize for quarterly earnings because their investors will leave for ones that do.

Recognizing Moloch is half the battle. The other half: opting out where you can, contributing to coordination mechanisms that escape the trap (regulation, professional norms, treaties), and at minimum, not adding fuel. You will not solve a Moloch problem with individual virtue. The structure has to change.

Personal versionIf you've ever felt forced into a competition you didn't want to be in — for jobs, for housing, for status — that's Moloch. The exit is usually sideways, not forward.

The danger is not that AI develops its own desires. The danger is that we will use AI to compete against each other so effectively that we'll end up in a Moloch world we never chose.

Scott Alexander · paraphrased

07.4Complex systems & emergent behavior

Most consequential decisions happen in complex systems — markets, ecosystems, social networks, large organizations — where the whole behaves in ways the parts don't.

Nonlinearity. Effects are not proportional to causes. Push the lever twice as hard and you might get four times the response, or no response, or a catastrophic phase change. The same intervention works at one scale and fails at another. Linear thinking misleads here constantly.

Power laws. A few things matter enormously; most things barely matter at all. 20% of customers drive 80% of revenue. The top 1% of content gets 99% of attention. The top 0.1% of code commits cause most outages. Strategic clarity comes from finding the vital few and ignoring the trivial many.

Optimization vs. redundancy. Efficient systems are fragile. Redundant systems are robust. Just-in-time supply chains were beautifully optimized until 2020. The pandemic and shipping disruptions revealed how much fragility had been silently accumulating in the name of efficiency. Slack and redundancy are not waste; they are insurance.

07.5Evolution & cultural learning

Evolution is not just about biology. Any system with variation, selection, and inheritance evolves: ideas, companies, technologies, cultures. Joseph Henrich's The Secret of Our Success argues that humans dominated the planet not through individual intelligence but through cumulative cultural evolution — the accumulated practical knowledge encoded in our traditions, techniques, and institutions.

The implication for decisions: tradition encodes information you don't have. When a culture has been doing something a particular way for 500 years, that's not necessarily backwardness — it's often accumulated wisdom about what works in this particular context, even when no individual can articulate why. Chesterton's Fence: before tearing down a fence whose purpose is unclear, you should at least understand why it was put up.

This cuts both ways. Some traditions are just stale conventions that survived past their useful date. The skill is distinguishing load-bearing customs from decorative ones — usually by asking: "If I remove this, what mechanism predicts the failure mode?" If you can't answer, the tradition probably encodes a lesson you haven't recovered yet.

LindyTaleb's "Lindy effect": the longer a non-perishable thing has survived, the longer it can be expected to survive. Books, ideas, institutions in the Lindy zone get the benefit of the doubt.

07.6Antifragility — building to gain from disorder

Taleb's most useful contribution. He distinguishes three responses to volatility:

  • Fragile things break under stress. A wine glass. A leveraged trade. A career built on one employer.
  • Resilient things survive stress unchanged. A diamond. A stable currency. A diversified income.
  • Antifragile things improve under stress. Muscles. Immune systems. Evolution. Skill under pressure.

Most things are fragile or resilient. Antifragility is rare and incredibly valuable. The lesson for design: build systems — and lives — that gain from disorder rather than merely surviving it.

Via negativa. The reliable path to antifragility is subtractive, not additive. We know what makes things fragile (debt, single points of failure, concentrated dependencies, lying to yourself). Removing fragility is more reliable than adding strength. Don't try to predict the storm. Build the boat that doesn't sink.

The barbell, revisited. Combine very safe positions with very speculative ones — avoid the middle. 85% in extreme safety, 15% in high-variance bets. This shape is antifragile: the safe side absorbs shocks while the speculative side captures upside. A career equivalent: stable day job + ambitious side projects, not a "promising" mid-tier role with neither security nor breakout potential.

Convexity. Prefer situations with bounded downside and unbounded upside. Most of life's biggest wins come from convex bets — small experiments where the worst case is "wasted a weekend" and the best case is "changed my career." The reverse is concave: bounded upside, unbounded downside (one more drink at the party, one more leveraged trade, one more late-night email). Sort your habits by which curve they're on. Eliminate the concave ones first.

07.7Meta-patterns — the six that tie it all together

The same shape shows up across every module of this manual. Once you see it, you see it everywhere.

PATTERN · 01
Asymmetry beats accuracy

You don't need to be right often. You need your rights to pay more than your wrongs cost. Hunt for convex setups. Skip symmetric ones.

PATTERN · 02
Survive first, optimize second

No EV calculation overrides ruin. Make sure the worst case keeps you in the game; then play to win. Buffett's rule one is the master pattern.

PATTERN · 03
Outside view first

Before your specific situation, check the reference class. The base rate is doing most of the work. Your case is less special than it feels.

PATTERN · 04
Decisions, not outcomes

Judge yourself by the quality of your process, not the result. Good decisions sometimes fail and bad decisions sometimes succeed. Don't learn the wrong lesson from either.

PATTERN · 05
Incentives over intentions

Map the incentives, predict the behavior. Trust people's structural position more than their stated motives. This applies to you too.

PATTERN · 06
Compound, don't interrupt

The biggest gains come from not stopping. Money, skill, reputation, health — all compound. Most life regrets are about interruptions, not slow starts.

The work is not building a perfect system. The work is making fewer of the same five mistakes — for decades, on purpose, in writing.

Ben · Working Manual
Try this today

Identify one Moloch trap you're currently inside — a competition you didn't choose, an optimization you can't escape, a metric that's eating the thing it was supposed to measure. Then ask the only real question: what's the sideways exit? Not "how do I win this game," but "how do I stop playing it."

Reference · A

Glossary.

The 28 concepts in this manual, defined for fast recall. Skim before any consequential decision.

Anchoring
The first number you see biases all subsequent estimates — even when irrelevant. Generate your own estimate before seeing anyone else's.
Antifragile
A system that gains from disorder. Beyond robust (which survives unchanged) and fragile (which breaks). Build for antifragility through redundancy and convex exposures.
Base rate
How often something happens in the relevant reference class, ignoring specifics. The number most people forget to multiply by. Almost always the dominant term.
Bayesian update
Adjusting belief = prior odds × likelihood ratio. The mechanical procedure for combining evidence with prior probability. The whole foundation of clear thinking under uncertainty.
Brier score
Mean squared error of probabilistic predictions vs. outcomes. Standard calibration metric. Lower is better; superforecasters land around 0.15.
Circle of competence
Buffett: the set of subjects where you can genuinely outperform an average smart person. Size matters less than knowing where the boundary is. Most catastrophic decisions happen just outside it.
Ergodicity
A property of systems where the average across many people equals the average over time for one person. When it fails — which is whenever ruin is possible — group EV stops applying to individuals.
Expected value (EV)
Σ (probability × payoff) across all outcomes. The mathematically correct way to evaluate a bet — if you can survive the losses along the way.
Fat-tailed
A distribution where extreme events drive most outcomes. Most causally-linked systems (wealth, fame, pandemics, hits) are fat-tailed. Bell-curve intuitions catastrophically fail here.
Goodhart's Law
When a measure becomes a target, it stops being a good measure. Every metric corrodes itself once people optimize for it.
Ideological Turing Test
Caplan's test: can you state an opposing view so well that sincere proponents would mistake you for one of them? If not, you don't understand it well enough to disagree productively.
Inversion
Munger's tool. Instead of "how do I succeed?", ask "what would guarantee failure?" and avoid those things. Inverted questions are almost always more tractable.
Kelly criterion
Mathematically optimal bet size: (p × b − q) / b. In practice, use half-Kelly to account for estimation error in p. Full Kelly is more aggressive than your real edge supports.
Kill criteria
Duke's fix for the sunk cost fallacy. Decide quitting conditions while still cold and clear; let them do the work when you'd otherwise be emotionally compromised.
Lollapalooza
Munger's term for several biases pushing the same direction simultaneously. The combined force is explosive — bubbles, panics, and cults are usually lollapaloozas.
Margin of safety
Graham's principle: don't aim for the boundary of what works — leave room for error. Build the bridge for 30,000 pounds and drive 10,000-pound trucks across it.
Moloch
Multipolar trap. A configuration where each actor is forced by competition to sacrifice something they value. No villain, just incentives. Exit is usually sideways.
Mr. Market
Graham's parable: imagine the market as a moody partner who shows up daily with a price. Trade with him only when his quotes suit you; otherwise ignore him.
Optionality
Choices that keep future doors open. Nonlinear upside with capped downside. The barbell is the canonical structure: ultra-safe + highly speculative, nothing in the middle.
Outside view
Estimating using a reference class of similar situations rather than your specific case. Far harder to defeat than the inside view. Almost always weighted too lightly.
Pre-mortem
Klein's tool. Imagine the decision has failed twelve months from now; write the obituary. Generates a richer failure list than "what could go wrong?" because hindsight bias gets reversed.
Regression to the mean
Extreme outcomes are partly luck; the luck doesn't repeat. Expect performances to drift back to the average. Don't credit your last intervention.
Resulting
Duke's term for judging a decision by its outcome rather than by its quality given the information available. The single most common decision-evaluation error.
Satisficer
Simon's term: someone who picks the first option clearing a threshold. Counterintuitively outperforms maximizers in happiness and often in outcome.
Second-order effects
What happens because of what happens. Most competitive advantage comes from consistently thinking one or two orders ahead. Most people don't.
Skin in the game
Taleb: people who bear consequences from their advice should be trusted more than those who don't. Asymmetric exposure produces asymmetric truthfulness.
Status quo bias
Treating "do nothing" as a non-decision. It isn't. Inaction is an active choice with its own expected value.
Steelmanning
Actively constructing the strongest version of a view you disagree with. The bar: a smart proponent would recognize your version as their best case.
Sunk cost fallacy
Letting past investment influence forward-looking decisions. The only relevant question is: starting from scratch today, would I commit this? Past spend is gone either way.

Further reading.

Twelve books. If you read three of these in the next year, every chapter of this manual will deepen.

01
Thinking, Fast and Slow
Daniel Kahneman
The foundational text. The two-systems framing, the catalog of biases, and the intellectual humility that comes from a Nobel laureate revising his own work decades later.
02
Superforecasting
Philip E. Tetlock & Dan Gardner
Twenty years of forecasting tournament data, distilled. The closest thing we have to a workout plan for the skill of being right about the future.
03
Thinking in Bets
Annie Duke
A former professional poker player on the discipline of separating decision quality from outcome quality. The clearest book on resulting ever written.
04
Quit
Annie Duke
The companion volume on knowing when to stop. The kill-criteria framework lives here. Reads short, hits hard.
05
The Scout Mindset
Julia Galef
On the difference between defending positions and actually trying to be right. Galef is one of the clearest writers on epistemics working today.
06
Antifragile
Nassim Nicholas Taleb
Idiosyncratic, occasionally infuriating, often profound. The framing of fragile / robust / antifragile alone is worth the price of the book.
07
Poor Charlie's Almanack
Charles T. Munger
A lifetime of decision wisdom from Buffett's partner. Worth owning physically and rereading sections every few years. The mental-models chapter alone is foundational.
08
Decisive
Chip Heath & Dan Heath
The most practical of the popular decision books. The WRAP framework (widen options, reality-test, attain distance, prepare to be wrong) is genuinely useful.
09
The Signal and the Noise
Nate Silver
Silver applies forecasting and Bayesian thinking to elections, weather, baseball, poker, and pandemics. The case studies are what stick.
10
How Big Things Get Done
Bent Flyvbjerg & Dan Gardner
The empirical case for the outside view. Flyvbjerg has the largest database of megaproject outcomes in existence and it's brutal reading for anyone who plans things.
11
The Secret of Our Success
Joseph Henrich
Anthropology that quietly rewires how you think about tradition, culture, and what "rational individual" even means. Pairs unusually well with the antifragility material.
12
The Precipice
Toby Ord
Decision theory applied to existential risk. The clearest treatment of how to think about very large stakes under deep uncertainty — the limit case of everything in this manual.
